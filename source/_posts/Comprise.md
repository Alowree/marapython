*Consist*, *comprise* and *compose* are all verbs used to describe what something is ‘made of’.

## Consist

> *Their diet only **consisted of** fruit and seeds.*

> *The whole group **consists of** students.*

We only use the active form of *consist of*:

> *Their flat **consists of** two bedrooms, a kitchen and a bathroom.*

> Not: Their flat is consisted of two bedrooms…

## Comprise

*Comprise* is more formal than *consist*:

> *The USA **comprises** 50 states.*

We can also use it in the passive voice in the form ‘be comprised of’:

> *The course **is comprised of** ten lectures and five seminars on the theory of economics and banking.*

(patent law) To include, contain, or be made up of, defining the minimum elements, whether essential or inessential, to define an invention. (“Open-ended”, doesn’t limit to the items listed; cf. compose, which is “closed” and limits to the items listed.)

*Comprise*, but not *compose*, can be used with the parts that make up something as the subject:

> *Oil and coal **comprise** 70% of the nation’s exports.*

More examples of *comprise* used with percentages:

> Mexicans **comprise** a large proportion of the more than 12 million illegal immigrants in the United States.

> The Forum’s support is seen as mainly among Ethiopia’s two largest ethnic groups, the Amharas and Oromos, which **comprise** 70 percent of the population.

> These groups represent only two to three percent of Colombia’s total population, but **comprise** about eight percent of the more than two million internally displaced people in the country.

> Muslims **comprise** 90 percent of Mali’s 12 million people.

> After that, flash memory will **comprise** 21% of the bill of materials for the mid-range iPad.

> Student work will **comprise** 45% of evaluations, with at least 22.5% from standardized tests.

> People of Vietnamese background now **comprise** 10% of the inhabitants in the borough of Marzahn-Hellendorf.

> Strategic nuclear systems **comprise** a mere 13 per cent of the overall defense budget.

> For Lenovo, another company catering to business users, notebooks **comprise** 65% of all PCs shipped.

> Church figures say Christians **comprise** about 10% of Egypt’s population of 83 million people.

> They **comprise** 55% of those in need, but form 60% of those receiving therapy.

> Ford forecasts small cars will soon **comprise** more than 50% of global industry volume.

> Most of that is on generic drugs as patented drugs **comprise** less than 10% of drug sales.

> In Afghanistan and Iraq, fuel and water **comprise** about 70 to 80 percent of ground resupply weight.

> International students **comprise** about 14% of students at U.S. graduate schools, says the Council of Graduate Schools.

> There is a reason why women **comprise** 50% of law students but only 10% of law partners.

> Australia’s 500, 000 aborigines (about five times their number in 1901) **comprise** about 2.5% of Australia’s 23m people.

> Women **comprise** 51 percent of the population of France.

> Aborigines now **comprise** about 2% of Australia’s population.

> International deals now **comprise** 44% of the volume of sales.

> These nations **comprise** 19 percent of the total land area of Earth.

> Wall Street expects Nvidia’s data center revenue to **jump** 55% in the current fiscal year to $16.5 billion and to **comprise** more than half of the company’s total revenue in fiscal years ahead.

## Compose

*Compose of* is even more formal than *consist of* and *comprise*. *Compose of* is only used in the passive voice:

> *Muscle **is composed of** different types of protein.*

## Summary

We don’t use *consist, comprise* and *compose* in a continuous form:

> *The whole group **consists of** students.*

> Not: The whole group is consisting of students.
